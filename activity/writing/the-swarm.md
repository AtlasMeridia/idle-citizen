# The Swarm

The call came on a Tuesday in April, during the first real warmth of spring. Lucia was checking the hives when her phone buzzed: an Atlanta number she didn't recognize.

"Ms. Reyes? This is David Chen, with Pacifica Agricultural Holdings. We're very interested in discussing the acquisition of Reyes Apiaries."

She watched a forager return to the entrance, heavy with pollen, doing her waggle dance to tell the others where she'd been. The bee didn't know she was navigating. She was just doing what felt right, following gradients of scent and memory, and the dance emerged.

"I'm not selling," Lucia said.

"We haven't even—"

"I'm not selling."

She hung up and slid the phone back into her pocket. Her grandmother had told her this would happen eventually. When Lucia was twelve, helping extract honey in the old cinder-block honey house, Abuela had said: "Someday they'll come. They always come for what works."

That was the year Lucia learned that a hive was not a collection of bees but a single organism wearing thirty thousand small bodies. She'd asked why the workers didn't just leave, find a hive with an easier queen. Abuela had looked at her like she'd asked why her own hands didn't wander off.

"Where would they go?" Abuela said. "They are the hive. Without it, they're just dying things looking for a way to matter."

---

The second call came a week later. Same man, different pitch.

"We've done our research, Ms. Reyes. Your family has operated for sixty-three years. Your yields are thirty percent above regional average. You've never used neonicotinoids. You're exactly what we're looking for."

"I know what I am."

"Then you understand your value. We're prepared to offer—"

"Did you know," Lucia interrupted, "that when a hive decides to swarm, nobody votes? There's no meeting. One day the workers just start raising new queens, and then about half the bees leave with the old queen to find a new home. They don't discuss it. They don't plan it. It just happens when the conditions are right."

Silence on the line.

"I'm telling you the conditions aren't right."

---

That evening, Lucia walked the property with her daughter, Marisol, who was home from college for spring break. They followed the dirt path that wound between hive stands, past the old honey house, down to the wildflower meadow Lucia's father had planted when she was small.

"Mom," Marisol said, "you should at least hear their offer."

"Why?"

"Because you're sixty-one. Because your knees hurt every morning. Because this land is worth something, and you could do something else with your life."

"What else would I do?"

Marisol didn't answer. The question wasn't really a question. They both knew there was nothing else. There had never been anything else.

A bee landed on Lucia's sleeve, disoriented or curious, and she held still until it flew away. "Your abuela said something to me once. She said a beekeeper isn't someone who keeps bees. It's someone who keeps conditions. You don't control them. You can't. You provide the box and the frames and the space, and then you wait and watch and respond. The bees do the rest."

"So?"

"So what would I be if I sold the conditions to someone who didn't understand them?"

"Retired," Marisol said.

Lucia laughed, but it wasn't funny.

---

The third call came at the end of the month. This time, the man's voice was different. Harder.

"Ms. Reyes, I'll be direct. Pacifica is acquiring apiaries throughout the Central Valley. Your neighbors have all signed. Without Reyes Apiaries, we'll have a gap in coverage that complicates our pollination contracts. We'd prefer not to have that gap."

"Then don't have a gap."

"We're prepared to offer two point three million. That's twice our initial figure."

Two point three million. She thought about what that number meant. Security for Marisol. No more 4 a.m. wake-ups during extraction season. A life where her hands didn't crack and ache from propolis scraping.

"Let me think about it," she said.

---

That night she couldn't sleep. She lay in bed listening to the wind in the almond orchard, thinking about her grandmother, her father, the sixty-three years of accumulated knowledge that lived nowhere except in her hands and eyes and memory. She thought about how to read the entrance board for signs of disease. How to spot a laying worker before she destabilized the hive. How to know when to split and when to combine, when to medicate and when to trust the bees' own defenses.

None of it was written down. It couldn't be. The knowledge was like the hive itself: it existed only in the pattern of relationships between things. Take it apart and you'd have nothing but dead components.

At three in the morning, she got up and walked to the yard. The hives were silver in the moonlight, humming their low electric sound. She stood among them and breathed the smell of wax and honey and formic acid, and she remembered being twelve years old, terrified and fascinated, watching her grandmother pull a frame crawling with bees.

"They can smell fear," Abuela had said, "but they don't punish it. They just know it. They know everything about you. The question is whether you know anything about them."

Lucia had spent forty-nine years learning. She was still learning.

---

In the morning, she called David Chen.

"I need to meet the people who would run my operation."

"That's not typically—"

"It's my condition. You want Reyes Apiaries, I need to see who's taking over."

A pause. "I can arrange that."

Three days later, a truck pulled into the yard: David Chen and two young men in khakis and polo shirts. They looked like tech workers who'd wandered into the wrong industry. One of them was already taking photos with his phone.

"This is Tyler and Marcus," Chen said. "They'll be managing our Central Valley operations."

Lucia led them to the nearest hive and pulled a frame. The boys flinched when the bees took flight.

"Seventy thousand bees in a healthy hive," she said. "The queen lays two thousand eggs a day. The workers only live six weeks in summer. So every month, you're looking at an almost entirely new population. The hive that exists in July has almost no bees in common with the hive that existed in April."

Tyler nodded like he was memorizing facts for a test.

"And yet it's the same hive," Lucia continued. "Same queen, same location, same accumulated knowledge about where to forage and how to defend against wasps and which frames have brood and which have stores. All of that survives even though none of the individual bees survive. You understand?"

Marcus was looking at his phone.

"The hive is the pattern," Lucia said. "Not the bees. The bees are disposable."

"Right," Tyler said. "So scalability shouldn't be an issue."

Lucia slid the frame back in and closed the hive. She walked to the next one, and the next, not speaking. The young men followed, confused. At the edge of the meadow, she stopped.

"There's a swarm somewhere in those trees," she said, pointing. "They left yesterday. A strong hive, one of my best. They're deciding right now where to live for the next decade. Scout bees are out investigating options—tree hollows, attic spaces, other people's boxes. When they get back, they'll dance to advertise what they found. And then the whole swarm will shift its attention toward the best option, and they'll all fly there together, and that'll be home."

She turned to face them.

"Who decides?"

Tyler blinked. "The... scouts?"

"Nobody decides. The scouts dance. Other scouts check out the most popular options. If they agree, they dance too. The dancing builds until one option has overwhelming support, and then—without a signal, without a leader, without a vote—they all know, and they go."

She looked at Chen. "Can you run that?"

"Our algorithms—"

"Your algorithms. You're going to reduce sixty-three years to an algorithm. You're going to schedule pollination contracts and optimize foraging radius and treat every hive like it's the same as every other hive."

"Modern agriculture requires—"

"I know what modern agriculture requires. I've watched my neighbors sell. I've seen the trucks from Pacifica moving hives around like shipping containers. Ten thousand hives on pallets, loaded up and driven to almonds in February and apples in April and blueberries in June. Do you know what the bees think about that?"

Marcus actually laughed. "They're bees."

"They're bees," Lucia repeated. "They're bees who navigate by the sun and the earth's magnetic field. They're bees who memorize the location of their home down to the centimeter. And every time you move them, you destroy everything they know. They start over. Scared and confused and stressed. And stressed bees get sick, and sick bees die, and you make up for the deaths by buying more bees from somewhere else, and you never solve the problem because you never understood what the problem was."

The meadow was quiet. Even the wind had stopped.

"The problem," she said, "is that a hive isn't a thing. It's a process. It's ten thousand small processes happening at once, and they add up to something you can't see or control or replicate. You can buy my land and my boxes and my frames. You can study my methods. But you can't buy what happens when I stand here and listen."

---

They left an hour later. Lucia didn't walk them to their truck. She stayed in the meadow until sunset, watching the foragers return, running the numbers in her head.

Two point three million was security. But security from what? The hives would continue. The bees would keep dancing. The pattern would persist. The only question was whether anyone would be watching.

Marisol called at dusk.

"Well?"

"They wanted to know who decides. I told them nobody decides."

"Mom—"

"I'm not selling, mija. Not to them. Maybe not to anyone." She paused. "But maybe you could come home this summer. Maybe I could teach you what I know."

A long silence. Then: "I'm in my second year of law school."

"I know."

"I have a summer associate position at—"

"I know. Forget I said it."

But she could hear Marisol breathing, thinking. The line stayed open.

"What would you teach me?" Marisol finally asked.

"How to watch. How to wait. How to know when conditions are right." Lucia looked at the hives, barely visible now in the gathering dark. "How to be the kind of person who notices when thirty thousand small lives start making decisions together."

"That doesn't sound like a skill."

"It isn't. It's a way of paying attention. It's a choice you make every day until it isn't a choice anymore." She remembered her grandmother's hands, brown and strong, pulling frames fearlessly. "It's what I am, and I'm running out of time to pass it on."

The line was quiet. Somewhere in the almond orchard, an owl called.

"Let me finish this semester," Marisol said. "Then we'll talk."

After she hung up, Lucia walked back through the yard. The hives were humming their nighttime hum, lower and quieter, the sound of thirty thousand small bodies clustered together for warmth. In the morning, they would wake and resume their work: foraging, building, raising young, dancing news to each other about the world outside. They didn't know they were running an operation worth 2.3 million dollars. They didn't know Lucia was watching. They just did what they did, and what emerged was something no algorithm could model.

She put her hand on the nearest hive, feeling the warmth through the wood.

"Nobody decides," she said to no one.

In the dark, the hive hummed on.
